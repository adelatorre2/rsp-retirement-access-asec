\section{Introduction}

This exercise constructs annual measures of retirement plan \emph{access} and \emph{participation} among private-sector wage-and-salary workers ages 20--64 using the Current Population Survey Annual Social and Economic Supplement (CPS ASEC) from 2010 through 2024. Access refers to whether an employer offers a retirement plan, while participation refers to whether the worker is included in that plan. Using IPUMS-CPS microdata, I construct weighted annual estimates and document the sample construction and measurement choices used to produce a transparent and replicable time series of retirement plan coverage.

\section{Data and Sample Construction}

The analysis uses person-level microdata from the Current Population Survey Annual Social and Economic Supplement (CPS ASEC) obtained through the IPUMS-CPS harmonized extract system. IPUMS provides consistently coded variables and documentation that allow comparable analysis across CPS samples over time (\cite{IPUMSCPS}). The CPS ASEC is an annual nationally representative survey conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau and the Bureau of Labor Statistics that collects detailed information on employment, income sources, and retirement-related characteristics of individuals in the United States (\cite{bureauAnnualSocialEconomic}).

The extract used for this exercise includes survey years 2010 through 2024 and contains the key demographic, labor-market, and retirement-plan variables required to construct measures of retirement plan access and participation. Because the CPS ASEC is a repeated cross-section rather than a longitudinal panel for this application, each survey year is treated as an independent nationally representative cross section. The pooled dataset is used only to construct consistent annual estimates.

The analytic sample is constructed in four steps. First, the sample is restricted to survey years 2010--2024. Second, the sample is limited to working-age adults ages 20--64. Third, I retain only individuals who are employed at the time of the survey using the CPS employment-status variable \texttt{EMPSTAT}. Workers coded as \texttt{EMPSTAT = 10} ("at work") or \texttt{EMPSTAT = 12} ("has job, not at work last week") are classified as employed and included in the analytic sample.

Fourth, among employed individuals I restrict the sample to private-sector wage-and-salary workers using the class-of-worker variable \texttt{CLASSWKR}. In the IPUMS coding scheme, private wage-and-salary workers correspond to codes 21 (private, for-profit wage and salary), 22 (private, nonprofit wage and salary), and 23 (private wage and salary, other). Government workers, members of the armed forces, unpaid family workers, and self-employed workers are excluded from the analytic sample. This restriction aligns the denominator with the conceptual object of interest: employer-sponsored retirement plans offered by private-sector employers.

After applying these restrictions, the analytic sample consists of private-sector wage-and-salary workers ages 20--64 observed in the CPS ASEC from 2010 through 2024. The resulting pooled dataset contains more than eight hundred thousand person-year observations, with annual sample sizes typically ranging from roughly 45,000 to 63,000 individuals depending on the survey year.

All estimates are weighted using the CPS ASEC person weight \texttt{ASECWT}. Applying this weight ensures that the resulting statistics represent the population of private-sector wage-and-salary workers in the United States rather than the raw survey sample. The use of person weights is standard practice when producing descriptive statistics from CPS ASEC microdata (\cite{bureauAnnualSocialEconomic}).

\section{Measuring Retirement Plan Access and Participation}

Retirement plan access and participation are measured using the CPS ASEC retirement coverage questions harmonized in the IPUMS-CPS dataset. Access captures whether a worker’s employer offers a pension or retirement plan, while participation captures whether the worker is included in such a plan.

In the CPS ASEC, respondents who are employed are asked whether their employer offers a pension or retirement plan for any of its employees. Workers who report that their employer offers a plan are classified as having retirement plan \emph{access}. Respondents are then asked whether they are included in the employer’s plan. Workers who report that they are included are classified as \emph{participants}.

Formally, the access rate is defined as the share of workers in the analytic sample who report that their employer offers a retirement plan. The participation rate is defined as the share of workers who report that they are included in such a plan. Both measures are computed annually using the CPS ASEC person weights so that estimates represent the population of private-sector wage-and-salary workers in the United States.


\section{Estimation Approach}

Annual estimates of retirement plan access and participation are computed using weighted descriptive statistics. For each survey year, the access rate is calculated as the weighted share of workers in the analytic sample who report that their employer offers a retirement plan. The participation rate is calculated as the weighted share of workers who report that they are included in a retirement plan.

All estimates use the CPS ASEC person weight \texttt{ASECWT}. This weight adjusts for the CPS sampling design and ensures that the resulting estimates are representative of the national population of private-sector wage-and-salary workers ages 20--64. Applying the person weight is standard practice when producing descriptive statistics from CPS ASEC microdata.

Observations with \texttt{PENSION = 0} (NIU) are treated as having no access and no participation so that the denominator remains the full analytic sample of private-sector wage-and-salary workers. This choice matches the replication outputs and provides a consistent annual base for the reported rates.


\section{CPS ASEC Redesign in 2015}

The interpretation of the resulting time series requires attention to changes in the CPS ASEC questionnaire. Beginning with the 2014 survey year, the Census Bureau introduced a redesigned income questionnaire for part of the sample, and beginning in 2015 the redesigned questionnaire was implemented for the full ASEC sample.

The redesign was intended to improve the reporting of income sources and retirement-related information. Because the measurement of retirement-plan coverage is conceptually linked to these broader income questions, the redesign may affect the comparability of responses before and after the change.

As a result, shifts in the estimated access and participation rates around the mid-2010s should be interpreted cautiously. Some portion of the observed change may reflect improvements in measurement rather than genuine changes in retirement-plan coverage among workers.


\section{Results}

The resulting estimates show that both retirement plan access and participation declined over the period from 2010 through 2024 among private-sector wage-and-salary workers ages 20--64. Access rates fell from slightly above one-half of workers at the beginning of the period to roughly two-fifths by the end of the sample, while participation rates followed a similar downward pattern. The gap between access and participation remains substantial throughout the period, indicating that a meaningful share of workers who have access to a retirement plan are not enrolled.
